AI is advancing so fast, new developments are coming out every day. It is hard to keep up with all the latest papers and models. When things are going too fast, all we need to do is study the change itself. We do not have time and energy to catch up with all the latest developments. We just need to study the driving force behind the change.

Exponentially cheaper compute and scaling of progressively more end-to-end models to leverage that compute.



AI research is not a fancy thing

Past 70 years of entire AI research can be summarized into developing progressively more general method with weaker modeling assumptions (inductive biases), and add more data and compute (scale up).

And that has been the recipe of entire AI research until now. Usually the more modeling assumptions, the less scalable the method is. More human input means less degree of freedom for the model to learn. An example of this is deep learning v.s. classical ML. Why is DL so much better than classical ML? It was more scalable. Because it has less structure, less inductive bias, and more degree of freedom.

With compute becoming cheaper (faster than we are becoming better researchers), the strucure of the model is becoming less and less important. That's why Decoder-only models (like ChatGPT, LLaMA, etc.) are so much better than Encoder-only models (like BERT, RoBERTa, etc.) because they are the least structured.



The less structure, the more scalable method is.

So why don't we just use the least structured, end-to-end model? It's infeasible. Instead we should find an "optimal" structure to add given the level of compute we have. In other words, we should find the most end-to-end structure that just started to work. Later on, we should revisit the structures we added and remove those that hinder scalability.
